## 3. Dave Gerhardt

The episode concludes with a look at what is currently working for Katelyn on LinkedIn and why she remains optimistic about the role of human-led marketing in the age of AI, noting that while automation increases the speed of content creation, true creative leadership remains a vital differentiator for brands (35:36-40:40).

## 4. Neil Patel
- My agency runs hundreds of tests after each Google update, and the businesses that fix these eight things right now are getting cited by AI, paying less for every click, and showing up everywhere; their competitors are disappearing. I'm Neil Patel, the co-founder of NP Digital, which just won performance marketing agency of the year by Adage. In this video, I'm going to show you exactly what changed, what is affecting both your SEO and your ad cost, and the step-by-step fixes to get ahead of it. Step one is to make your website fast (0:34). For 25 years, Google treated your website as a one-way for organic search and a completely different way for ads, but that separation is no longer viable. Google's AI now relies heavily on your website to create ads, determine relevance, and choose landing pages. If your page takes four or five seconds to load, people bounce before they convert, and the AI sees that low conversion rate and is less likely to spend budget sending people to that page. Go to Google Page Speed Insights to test your top landing pages and fix the slowest ones. Step two is to clean up words on your website (2:18). Because Google's AI is pulling from your headlines, title tags, and descriptions to build your ads, it needs high-quality raw material. If your headline is vague, like "our solutions" or "welcome to our website," the AI builds ads that nobody wants to click, but you still pay for the impressions. Ask yourself: if this headline showed up as a Google ad, would someone click it? If not, rewrite it to be specific and benefit-driven. Step three is to add real images and videos to your key pages (3:44). If you don't provide these, the system can autogenerate low-quality videos or use generic stock images to fill the gap. Get a clean, well-lit 15 to 30-second video and use real photos of your product, your team, or your results to give the AI quality material to work with across every channel. Step four, for anyone selling products online, is to fix your product feed (4:46). The data you send to Google Merchant Center is the foundation of shopping ads. Rich data, including brand name, color, size, and materials, drastically improves ad relevance and quality score. Thin product data means the AI has less to work with, which shows up in your results. Step five is to help Google's AI understand what your website actually does by adding schema markup (5:59). Think of it as labels you add behind the scenes that tell Google exactly what is on each page, which can lead to richer ad extensions like star ratings or FAQ answers, boosting your click-through rates. Step six is to stop creating a bunch of small pages on similar topics and instead build one deep page that covers everything (7:06). When Google's AI sees a strong pillar page, it's more likely to map broad search intent to the right landing page, outperforming dozens of scattered posts. Step seven is to show Google and your customers that real people trust you by building trust signals (8:39). Focus on demonstrating genuine expertise, experience, and authority (E-E-A-T). The new game is real credibility and real reviews rather than trying to shortcut this with spammy backlinks. Finally, step eight is to get your SEO team and your paid team in the same room (9:58). Most companies keep these teams in silos, but since Google's AI treats your website as one system, you should have them compare notes once a month. Have your SEO team build content around searches that drive paid conversions, and have them flag irrelevant searches that are wasting ad budget so the paid team can exclude them. This turns your website into a self-improving machine.
